Buisness Dining Etiquette


It is every business person’s dream to make it to an official business dinner, or to sit amongst the big shots as many people say. With some empathetic listening, persuasive conversation and some proper dinner etiquette, you are on your way to sealing the deal. Now the question you are faced with, how well do you understand business dinner etiquette? Like many of us, we have very little understanding until we are presented with the circumstance. Today however, I will be teaching you some fundamentals on business dinner etiquette. Here are some things to consider before, during and after your meeting.

Setting
Selecting your location is very critical and can affect the outcome of your meeting. You first want to make sure to choose something your familiar with so that no surprises may arise. Choose a place that is not overly expensive but yet provides tranquility and cleanliness. Anything otherwise can create distractions or make it hard to focus on the primary objective or target. You want to ensure that your prospect has your full attention and that no external noise or visuals disturbances take their focus elsewhere. If you are unaware of your client’s food choices, it is best to choose a place that is not limited in culture variation. For this reason, avoid sushi places unless you know your client’s food desires. Overall, be mindful of different eating habits and food choices. In other words, make sure the place you choose has a selection that caters to vegetarians, vegans, flexitarian, pescatarian, etc. The best in deciding where to eat is to do some research and find out what your client’s eating choices are before choosing a place. At the top of the website toolbar under restaurants, you will see suggested links to restaurants here in town that would be good places to consider for business dinners.

Before The Event
Prior to arriving to the meeting, ensure you eat something small to avoid feeling hunger and not allowing yourself to concentrate on your client. Although this is a dinner meeting, it is intended for open conversation and first impressions. As previously stated, you want to devote your attention on the client and not the food. Since you are the primary spokesperson, or the person in charge of arranging the meeting, be sure to make reservations to avoid awkward wait times in the lobby. In addition, arrive earlier than you client, this will demonstrate accountability and the ability to be punctual. Upon arriving, put your phone on silence and wait for you guests at your reserved table. Keep your phone at eyesight until they arrive, just in case they try to call for any reason. Do not put your phone on vibrate during the meeting as this too can cause a distraction or annoyance to the client. The last recommendation before attending the meeting is to determine what you will order to eat. Do not waste time in trying to read the menu there or allow yourself to be dumbfounded by the various choices. See Menu options for more details

First Impressions
First impressions are crucial because you do not get a second chance at a first encounter. Make sure to dress the part, introduce yourself with a firm handshake and greet them with genuine sincerity. Dressing the part can fluctuate depending on the level of business professionalism involved. For some examples, I have provided a link called business attire to help demonstrate the different types of appropriate clothing options. Once the meet and greet has been settled, ensure all personal belongings are hidden away out of hindsight. This will demonstrate cleanliness and avoid obstruction of view.

Menu Options
As previously recommended, know what you want to eat before arriving to the meeting. When choosing what to eat, consider price and cleanliness. You want to avoid overly expensive meals, strong odors, and something that is not messy. Messy can be anything with too much sauce, finger foods, foods that stain, foods that stay in your teeth or anything that requires large bite sizes. When ordering, do not be complicated and try to choose foods like chicken or salads which have less add-ons to take off and do not have caution temperatures. Allow your guest to order first, this could also open up ideas for you as well.

Table Manners
There are two major concepts to understand when dealing with table manners. The first thing to consider, is understanding the usage of each utensil, and secondly would be how to properly eat in front of others. Some of these things may seem standard but quite important to reiterate or establish for those who may still be questioning proper etiquette. Many restaurants use different types of utensils and it can be quite confusing. Figure 1 helps to demonstrate what each utensil is used for. In addition, a video in the toolbar has been provided for further explanation. The video provides both topics on proper table manners and the correct usage of each utensil or silverware. Some general advice would be, remember to eat at a slower pace than your use to, do not eat with your mouth full and do not take big bites.

Closing The Meeting
Since you are seeking the partnership of the client, make the move to pay. Even if you did not initiate the meeting, it is good practice and or manner to attempt to pay. Also thank your guest for attending and taking the time to talk business. Make sure to thank them by name and give them eye contact. It is also not a bad idea to thank them again through email. This will provide your contact information for any future business and a quick recap of your meeting.
